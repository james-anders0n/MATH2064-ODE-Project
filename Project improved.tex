\section{Abstract}

Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) are used to model dynamic processes in many disciplines, including engineering, physics, and biology. In epidemiology, systems of ODEs are commonly employed to simulate the spread of infectious diseases within a population. However, many realistic models are nonlinear and do not admit simple analytical solutions, making numerical methods essential for approximating their behaviour. Among the most widely used approaches are the Runge--Kutta (RK) methods, which offer improved accuracy compared to simpler methods such as Euler’s method.

This paper investigates the performance of several numerical methods for solving ordinary differential equations, with a particular focus on the Euler method, the second-order Runge--Kutta method (RK2), and the fourth-order Runge--Kutta method (RK4). The methods are first applied to a simple ODE with a known analytical solution to evaluate convergence behaviour and global error as a function of step size. They are then applied to the nonlinear Susceptible--Infected--Recovered (SIR) epidemic model to simulate the spread of disease within a population. The numerical solutions are evaluated in terms of accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency, providing insight into the relative performance of each method when modelling complex real-world systems. The results highlight the trade-off between numerical accuracy and computational cost, illustrating the advantages of higher-order Runge--Kutta methods for modelling nonlinear dynamical systems.

\section{Introduction}

Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) describe systems in which variables evolve over time according to specified rates of change. These equations are fundamental in many disciplines, including engineering (fluid flow and heat transfer systems), physics (oscillations and motion), and biology (population dynamics and epidemiology).

In many practical situations, ODE systems are nonlinear, coupled, and do not admit simple analytical solutions. As a result, numerical methods must be implemented to approximate their behaviour. These methods discretise the time domain and compute approximate solutions at discrete points, allowing complex systems to be analysed and predicted.

Within epidemiology, modelling the spread of infectious disease is a dynamic problem of significant importance. Mathematical models help predict outbreaks, estimate peak infection levels, and inform public health decisions. One of the most widely used models is the SIR model, which divides a population into susceptible, infected, and recovered compartments.

This report investigates the performance of several numerical methods for solving ODEs, with particular focus on Euler’s method, the second-order Runge--Kutta method (RK2), and the fourth-order Runge--Kutta method (RK4). The methods are first evaluated using a simple ODE with a known analytical solution, before being applied to the nonlinear SIR model. The accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency of each method are compared to determine their suitability for different types of problems.

\section{Theory}

\subsection{Ordinary Differential Equations}
An Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) describes the relationship between a function and its derivatives with respect to a single independent variable (in this case, time). A general first-order Initial Value Problem (IVP) is given by:

\begin{equation}
\frac{dy}{dt} = f(t,y), \quad y(t_0) = y_0,
\end{equation}
where $f(t,y)$ is a known function and $y(t)$ represents the unknown solution.
In many practical applications, particularly when $f(t,y)$ is nonlinear, analytical solutions can be extremely difficult or impossible to obtain. As a result, the solution must be approximated numerically by discretising time into steps of size $h$, producing a sequence of approximations $\{y_n\}$ at discrete points $t_n = t_0 + nh$.

\subsection{Derivation of Numerical Approximation Framework}

To construct a numerical scheme, we begin by integrating both sides of Equation (1) over a small interval $[t_n, t_{n+1}]$:
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} f(t,y(t)) \,dt.
\end{equation}

This integral cannot generally be evaluated analytically because the exact solution $y(t)$ is unknown. Therefore, the problem reduces to approximating this integral. The different numerical methods considered in this report---Euler’s method, RK2, and RK4---can all be interpreted as different approaches to approximating this integral, with varying levels of accuracy and computational cost.

\subsection{Euler's Method}

The simplest approach to approximating the integral is Euler’s method. We approximate $f(t,y)$ as constant over the interval using its value at the beginning of the step:
\begin{equation}
\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} f(t,y(t)) \, dt \approx h f(t_n, y_n).
\end{equation}

Substituting into Equation (2) gives Euler’s method:
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + h f(t_n, y_n).
\end{equation}

The solution is approximated by assuming that the slope remains constant over each time step, using the derivative evaluated at the beginning of the interval. Geometrically, this corresponds to advancing the solution along the tangent line at $(t_n, y_n)$. While this approach is simple and computationally inexpensive, it neglects curvature in the solution, leading to rapid accumulation of error. Consequently, Euler’s method is only first-order accurate and requires sufficiently small step sizes to maintain stability and accuracy.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis of Euler's Method}

Using a Taylor series expansion of the true solution,
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + h y'(t_n) + \frac{h^2}{2} y''(\xi),
\end{equation}
for some $\xi \in [t_n, t_{n+1}]$, Euler’s method neglects higher-order terms. The local truncation error is therefore $\mathcal{O}(h^2)$, which leads to a global error of $\mathcal{O}(h)$.

\subsection{Runge-Kutta Methods}

Runge--Kutta methods improve accuracy by evaluating $f(t,y)$ at multiple points within each time step, providing a more accurate approximation of the integral in Equation (2).

\subsubsection{Second-Order Runge--Kutta Method (RK2)}

A second-order approximation can be obtained by averaging the slope over the interval:
\begin{align}
k_1 &= f(t_n, y_n), \\
k_2 &= f(t_n + h, y_n + h k_1), \\
y_{n+1} &= y_n + \frac{h}{2}(k_1 + k_2).
\end{align}

The RK2 method improves upon Euler’s method by incorporating information about the slope at both the beginning and end of the interval. This allows the method to better capture variation in the slope across the step, improving the approximation of the solution. As a result, RK2 achieves second-order accuracy and provides significantly improved results compared to Euler’s method, with only a moderate increase in computational cost.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis of RK2}

The RK2 method has a local truncation error of $\mathcal{O}(h^3)$ and a global error of $\mathcal{O}(h^2)$, allowing larger step sizes to be used while maintaining good accuracy.

\subsubsection{Fourth-Order Runge--Kutta Method (RK4)}

A further refinement is given by the fourth-order Runge--Kutta method:
\begin{align}
k_1 &= f(t_n, y_n), \\
k_2 &= f\left(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_1\right), \\
k_3 &= f\left(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_2\right), \\
k_4 &= f(t_n + h, y_n + h k_3),
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4).
\end{equation}

The RK4 method evaluates the slope at four points within each time step and combines them using a weighted average. This enables the method to accurately capture higher-order behaviour of the solution, including curvature and variation in the slope. As a result, RK4 achieves fourth-order accuracy and typically produces highly accurate results even for relatively large step sizes.
This construction can also be interpreted as a quadrature rule for approximating the integral in Equation (2), where the weights are chosen to maximise accuracy up to fourth order.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis of RK4}

RK4 has a local truncation error of $\mathcal{O}(h^5)$ and a global error of $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$. Although it requires more function evaluations per step, its superior accuracy often leads to greater overall efficiency compared to lower-order methods.

\subsection{The SIR Model}

The Susceptible--Infected--Recovered (SIR) model describes the spread of infectious disease within a closed population. In this report, the model is written in normalised form, so that $S(t)$, $I(t)$, and $R(t)$ represent fractions of the total population:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $S(t)$: susceptible individuals,
    \item $I(t)$: infected individuals,
    \item $R(t)$: recovered individuals.
\end{itemize}

The governing equations are
\begin{align}
\frac{dS}{dt} &= -\beta S I, \\
\frac{dI}{dt} &= \beta S I - \gamma I, \\
\frac{dR}{dt} &= \gamma I,
\end{align}
where $\beta$ is the transmission rate and $\gamma$ is the recovery rate.

The term $\beta S I$ arises from the assumption that the rate of infection is proportional to both the number of susceptible and infected individuals, reflecting random interactions within the population. The recovery term $\gamma I$ assumes that infected individuals recover at a constant rate.

\subsection{Basic Reproduction Number}

The basic reproduction number is defined as
\begin{equation}
R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma},
\end{equation}
and represents the expected number of secondary infections produced by a single infected individual in a fully susceptible population. If $R_0 > 1$, the infection spreads, whereas if $R_0 < 1$, the disease dies out.

\subsection{Conservation of Population}

Summing the governing equations yields
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt}(S+I+R) = 0,
\end{equation}
which implies that the total population remains constant over time. In the normalised form used throughout this report,
\begin{equation}
S(t) + I(t) + R(t) = 1.
\end{equation}

This conservation law provides an important validation check for numerical solutions.

\subsection{Implications for Numerical Methods}

The SIR system is nonlinear and coupled, making it sensitive to numerical errors. Poor numerical schemes or excessively large step sizes can produce non-physical results, such as negative population values or violation of the conservation law. Therefore, it is essential to use numerical methods that are both accurate and stable when simulating epidemic dynamics.

\section{Numerical Methods}

In this section, the numerical schemes derived in the previous section are translated into practical algorithms. While the theory focused on the approximation of the integral form of the governing equations, the purpose here is to describe how these methods are implemented and applied in a computational setting.

Each method advances the solution iteratively using a fixed time step $h$, allowing the solution to be approximated over a specified interval. The differences between the methods arise from how the integral in Equation (2) is approximated, which directly impacts both accuracy and computational cost.

\subsection{Euler Method Implementation}

Euler’s method provides the simplest numerical implementation of the integral approximation introduced in the theory section. The solution is advanced using the slope evaluated at the beginning of each time step:
\[
y_{n+1} = y_n + h f(t_n, y_n).
\]

The method is implemented iteratively, starting from the initial condition $y_0$. At each time step, the derivative function is evaluated once, and the solution is updated accordingly.

Due to its simplicity, Euler’s method is computationally efficient and easy to implement. However, because it assumes the slope remains constant over the interval, it introduces relatively large numerical errors unless a sufficiently small step size is used. As a result, it is primarily useful as a baseline method for comparison with higher-order schemes.

\subsection{Second-Order Runge--Kutta Method (RK2)}

The second-order Runge--Kutta (RK2) method improves upon Euler’s method by incorporating information about the slope at multiple points within each time step, rather than relying solely on the slope at the beginning of the interval. This results in a more accurate approximation of the integral in Equation (2), and consequently a better approximation of the solution.

To construct the method, we again consider the integral form:
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} f(t,y(t)) \, dt.
\end{equation}

Instead of approximating the integrand as constant (as in Euler’s method), RK2 approximates the average slope over the interval by evaluating the derivative at both the beginning and the end of the step. First, an initial estimate of the slope is computed:
\begin{equation}
k_1 = f(t_n, y_n).
\end{equation}

This is used to predict a provisional value at the end of the interval:
\begin{equation}
y_n^{*} = y_n + h k_1.
\end{equation}

A second slope is then evaluated at this predicted point:
\begin{equation}
k_2 = f(t_n + h, y_n^{*}).
\end{equation}

The solution is then advanced using the average of these two slopes:
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{2}(k_1 + k_2).
\end{equation}

Geometrically, this method can be interpreted as averaging the slope at the beginning and the end of the interval, thereby accounting for variation in the derivative across the step.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis of RK2}

To analyse the error, we again consider the Taylor series expansion of the exact solution about $t_n$:
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + h y'(t_n) + \frac{h^2}{2} y''(t_n) + \frac{h^3}{6} y'''(\xi),
\end{equation}
for some $\xi \in [t_n, t_{n+1}]$.

We now expand the RK2 update. Using $k_1 = f(t_n, y_n) = y'(t_n)$, and noting that
\begin{equation}
k_2 = f(t_n + h, y_n + h k_1),
\end{equation}
we expand $k_2$ about $(t_n, y_n)$:
\begin{equation}
k_2 = y'(t_n) + h \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + h y'(t_n)\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} + \mathcal{O}(h^2).
\end{equation}

Combining terms, the RK2 update becomes
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + h y'(t_n) + \frac{h^2}{2} y''(t_n) + \mathcal{O}(h^3).
\end{equation}

Comparing with the exact Taylor expansion, we see that RK2 correctly reproduces all terms up to order $h^2$, and neglects terms of order $h^3$ and higher. Therefore, the local truncation error is
\begin{equation}
\tau_n = \mathcal{O}(h^3).
\end{equation}

Over $N \sim \frac{1}{h}$ steps, this accumulates to a global error of
\begin{equation}
E_N = \mathcal{O}(h^2).
\end{equation}

The RK2 method is second-order accurate. This represents a significant improvement over Euler’s method, as the error decreases quadratically with step size rather than linearly. In practical terms, this allows for larger step sizes to be used while maintaining a comparable level of accuracy. This behaviour will be examined later in the benchmark problem through convergence results.

\subsection{Fourth-Order Runge--Kutta Method (RK4)}

The fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) method further refines the approximation of the integral in Equation (2) by evaluating the slope at multiple intermediate points within each time step and combining them in a weighted average. This approach provides a highly accurate estimate of the average slope over the interval, allowing the method to capture both curvature and higher-order behaviour of the solution.

Starting again from the integral formulation,
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} f(t,y(t)) \, dt,
\end{equation}
the RK4 method approximates the integral using four slope evaluations.

First, the slope at the beginning of the interval is computed:
\begin{equation}
k_1 = f(t_n, y_n).
\end{equation}

A second slope is evaluated at the midpoint using $k_1$:
\begin{equation}
k_2 = f\left(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_1\right).
\end{equation}

A third slope is again evaluated at the midpoint, now using $k_2$:
\begin{equation}
k_3 = f\left(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_2\right).
\end{equation}

Finally, a fourth slope is evaluated at the end of the interval using $k_3$:
\begin{equation}
k_4 = f(t_n + h, y_n + h k_3).
\end{equation}

These slopes are combined in a weighted average to advance the solution:
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}\left(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4\right).
\end{equation}

The weighting reflects the relative importance of each slope in approximating the integral, with greater weight placed on the midpoint evaluations. Geometrically, RK4 can be interpreted as constructing a highly accurate estimate of the average slope over the interval by sampling the curve at chosen points. This allows the method to closely track the true trajectory of the solution even if the derivative varies significantly within a single step.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis of RK4}

To analyse the error, we again consider the Taylor series expansion of the exact solution:
\begin{equation}
y(t_{n+1}) = y(t_n) + h y'(t_n) + \frac{h^2}{2} y''(t_n) + \frac{h^3}{6} y'''(t_n) + \frac{h^4}{24} y^{(4)}(t_n) + \frac{h^5}{120} y^{(5)}(\xi),
\end{equation}
for some $\xi \in [t_n, t_{n+1}]$.

The RK4 method constructs its update by combining the four slope evaluations. Expanding each $k_i$ about $(t_n, y_n)$ and substituting into the update formula yields
\begin{equation}
y_{n+1} = y_n + h y'(t_n) + \frac{h^2}{2} y''(t_n) + \frac{h^3}{6} y'''(t_n) + \frac{h^4}{24} y^{(4)}(t_n) + \mathcal{O}(h^5).
\end{equation}

The RK4 update matches the Taylor expansion of the true solution up to terms of order $h^4$, with discrepancies only appearing at order $h^5$. The local truncation error is therefore
\begin{equation}
\tau_n = \mathcal{O}(h^5).
\end{equation}

Over $N \sim \frac{1}{h}$ steps, this leads to a global error of
\begin{equation}
E_N = \mathcal{O}(h^4).
\end{equation}

Therefore, the RK4 method is fourth-order accurate. This implies that the error decreases very rapidly as the step size is refined. In practical terms, halving the step size reduces the global error by approximately a factor of $16$, which is a substantial improvement over lower-order methods. Consequently, RK4 is able to achieve high accuracy with relatively large step sizes, making it particularly well-suited to nonlinear systems such as the SIR model. This improved performance is reflected in both the benchmark convergence results and the computational time comparison presented later.

\subsection{Implementation Summary}

Although the methods differ in their mathematical formulation, they are all implemented using a time-stepping procedure. For a given step size $h$, the total number of steps is
\[
N = \frac{T - t_0}{h}.
\]

At each step, the solution is updated using the formulas derived above. The primary difference between the methods lies in the number of function evaluations required:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Euler method: 1 evaluation per step,
    \item RK2 method: 2 evaluations per step,
    \item RK4 method: 4 evaluations per step.
\end{itemize}

This difference in computational effort per step is important when comparing runtime, and will be reflected later in the computational time graph.

For the SIR model, the methods are extended to a system of equations by representing the solution as a vector $\mathbf{y} = [S, I, R]^T$. The update formulas are then applied component-wise, allowing all variables to be advanced simultaneously.

This implementation framework allows direct comparison of accuracy, convergence, and computational cost across the different numerical methods.

\subsubsection{Computational Complexity}

The computational cost of each method can be analysed in terms of the number of function evaluations required. For a simulation with $N$ time steps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Euler method requires $N$ function evaluations,
    \item RK2 requires $2N$ function evaluations,
    \item RK4 requires $4N$ function evaluations.
\end{itemize}

Therefore, the computational complexity of all methods is $\mathcal{O}(N)$, but with different constant factors. This distinction is important in practice, as higher-order methods require more work per step but may achieve the same accuracy with significantly fewer steps. This trade-off between cost per step and convergence rate is central to selecting an appropriate numerical method.

\section{Examples and Applications}

To evaluate the performance of the numerical methods, two types of problems were considered: a benchmark differential equation with a known analytical solution and the nonlinear SIR epidemic model. The benchmark problem allows the numerical error and convergence behaviour to be quantified, while the SIR model demonstrates the application of the methods to a realistic coupled nonlinear system.

\subsection{Benchmark Problem}

The benchmark problem considered is
\[
\frac{dy}{dt} = -2y, \quad y(0)=1,
\]
which has the exact solution
\[
y(t)=e^{-2t}.
\]

This problem is particularly useful because the exact solution is known, allowing direct comparison with the numerical approximations. Euler’s method, RK2, and RK4 were applied over the interval $[0,2]$ using a range of step sizes.

To assess accuracy, the maximum global error was computed as
\[
E_h = \max_{t_n} \left| y_{\text{exact}}(t_n) - y_n \right|.
\]

The observed order of convergence was estimated using
\[
p \approx \frac{\log(E_h/E_{h/2})}{\log 2}.
\]

Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} compares the numerical and exact solutions for a representative step size. Euler’s method shows the largest deviation from the exact solution due to its first-order accuracy. RK2 provides a noticeable improvement by incorporating additional slope information, while RK4 produces results that are almost indistinguishable from the exact solution.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{comparison_plot.png}
\caption{Comparison of Euler, RK2, and RK4 solutions with the exact solution for the benchmark problem $y'=-2y$.}
\label{fig:comparison}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the convergence behaviour of the three methods on a log--log scale. The slopes of the curves clearly match the theoretical error orders derived earlier, with Euler exhibiting first-order convergence, RK2 exhibiting second-order convergence, and RK4 exhibiting fourth-order convergence. This agreement between observed and theoretical convergence rates provides strong verification of the correctness of the numerical implementations.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{convergence_plot.png}
\caption{Log--log convergence plot showing the dependence of maximum global error on step size for Euler, RK2, and RK4.}
\label{fig:convergence}
\end{figure}

These results confirm that higher-order methods provide significantly improved accuracy for a given step size. Euler’s method is simple and computationally efficient, but its first-order convergence means that the error decreases relatively slowly as the step size is refined. RK2 provides a clear improvement by reducing the global error quadratically with step size. RK4 performs best, with fourth-order convergence allowing very small errors to be achieved even for moderate step sizes. This demonstrates that a higher computational cost per step can be justified when it leads to substantially improved accuracy.

\subsection{Computational Time Comparison}

In addition to accuracy, the computational cost of each numerical method was assessed using the benchmark problem. The average runtime of Euler’s method, RK2, and RK4 was measured over repeated simulations using the same step size.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{timing_plot.png}
\caption{Average computational time for Euler, RK2, and RK4 on the benchmark problem.}
\label{fig:timing}
\end{figure}

The timing results are consistent with the structure of the methods. Euler’s method is the fastest because it evaluates the derivative only once per step, while RK2 and RK4 require two and four evaluations respectively. However, the benchmark convergence results show that accuracy improves much more rapidly for the higher-order methods. As a result, although RK4 has the highest computational cost per step, it can often achieve a given error tolerance with far fewer steps than Euler’s method. This highlights an important point: the cheapest method per step is not necessarily the most efficient method overall.

\subsection{SIR Model Simulation}

The SIR model was solved numerically using the RK4 method with parameters
\[
\beta = 0.35, \qquad \gamma = 0.10,
\]
and initial conditions
\[
S_0 = 0.95, \qquad I_0 = 0.05, \qquad R_0 = 0.
\]

The simulation was performed over the interval $[0,160]$ with step size $h=0.5$. The resulting compartment histories are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sir-rk4}.

Initially, the infected population increases rapidly as the disease spreads through the susceptible population. As the number of susceptible individuals decreases and recovery becomes dominant, the infected population reaches a peak before declining. Meanwhile, the recovered population increases monotonically and the susceptible population decreases continuously.

The numerical results indicate that the infected population reaches a maximum of approximately $I_{\max} \approx 0.36$ at around $t \approx 25$. This behaviour is consistent with the theoretical expectation, since the basic reproduction number is
\[
R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma} = 3.5 > 1,
\]
indicating that the infection spreads through the population before eventually declining.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{sir_rk4.png}
\caption{Time evolution of the susceptible, infected, and recovered populations obtained using the RK4 method.}
\label{fig:sir-rk4}
\end{figure}

The smooth behaviour of the numerical solution and the absence of non-physical oscillations indicate that the chosen step size is sufficiently small for RK4 to capture the epidemic dynamics reliably.
This behaviour reflects the depletion of the susceptible population, which reduces the effective reproduction number over time until the infection can no longer sustain itself.

\subsection{Validation of the Numerical Solution}

An important property of the SIR model is conservation of total population:
\[
S(t) + I(t) + R(t) = 1.
\]

The numerical solution satisfied this condition with a maximum deviation of less than $10^{-12}$, indicating excellent numerical stability and negligible accumulated error. This provides strong validation of both the implementation and the suitability of RK4 for this nonlinear system.

\subsection{Overall Comparison}

The benchmark and SIR results together demonstrate the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. Euler’s method is simple and fast but relatively inaccurate. RK2 improves the approximation significantly with a moderate increase in computational cost. RK4 is the most computationally expensive per step, but its superior convergence behaviour and stability make it the most effective method overall.

For nonlinear systems such as the SIR model, where errors can propagate through coupled variables, the improved robustness of higher-order methods becomes particularly important.

\section{Conclusion}

This report investigated the performance of Euler’s method, RK2, and RK4 for solving ordinary differential equations. Using a benchmark problem with known analytical solution, the expected convergence rates were confirmed and the trade-off between computational efficiency and numerical accuracy was demonstrated. Euler’s method was found to be the least accurate, RK2 provided a substantial improvement, and RK4 delivered the highest accuracy together with strong stability characteristics.

The methods were then applied to the nonlinear SIR epidemic model, where RK4 produced accurate, stable, and physically consistent solutions while preserving the total population. Overall, the results show that although higher-order methods require more work per time step, their improved convergence behaviour makes them more effective for solving nonlinear dynamical systems. This highlights the importance of selecting a numerical method based not only on implementation simplicity, but also on the accuracy and reliability required by the problem.
Among the methods considered, RK4 provides the best balance between accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency for the problems studied.
